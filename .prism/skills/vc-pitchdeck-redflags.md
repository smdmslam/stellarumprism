## Purpose

Many weak decks do not fail because the company is weak.
They fail because the presentation triggers investor skepticism, boredom, confusion, or disbelief.

This skill captures the most common red flags to watch for and fix.

Use it as a review filter after drafting.

---

## Red flag 1 — Product brochure mode

### What it looks like
- long lists of features
- screenshots or UI descriptions with no strategic meaning
- too much “what it does” and too little “why it matters”
- implementation detail presented as if it were investment logic

### Why it hurts
It makes the company feel like software, not a venture outcome.
Investors do not fund feature tours. They fund companies with leverage, market pull, and defensibility.

### Signs in language
- “our platform includes”
- “users can click”
- “the interface contains”
- “this dashboard allows”

### Fix
Translate every important feature into:
- value
- control point
- recurring workflow relevance
- moat
- expansion path

---

## Red flag 2 — No category-defining line

### What it looks like
The deck opens without clearly stating what kind of company this is.

### Why it hurts
If the investor cannot place the company quickly, the rest of the deck lands weakly.
The opening should create a frame, not a puzzle.

### Signs in language
- vague taglines
- poetic but unclear opening lines
- product names with no explanatory category
- mission language before category language

### Fix
Force a direct category line such as:
- the verification layer for AI-built software
- the control plane for software trust
- the production gate for machine-generated code

---

## Red flag 3 — Technical detail too early

### What it looks like
The deck leads with architecture, cell counts, benchmarks, frameworks, or implementation structure before the investor understands the problem or why the company matters.

### Why it hurts
Technical depth is useful only after strategic meaning is clear.
Too-early implementation detail makes the company feel narrow and founder-centric.

### Signs in language
- counts of tools or modules on slide 1 or 2
- model pricing, runtimes, and protocol details before the pain is clear
- detailed architecture before category, wedge, or market shift

### Fix
Delay detail until:
- proof
- moat
- product surface
- diligence follow-up

Lead instead with:
- market shift
- painful problem
- insight
- company role

---

## Red flag 4 — Generic AI startup language

### What it looks like
The deck uses fashionable but empty phrases.

### Why it hurts
Generic AI wording signals lack of strategic clarity and sounds interchangeable with hundreds of other decks.

### Signs in language
- revolutionary AI platform
- empowering developers
- next-generation workflow assistant
- intelligent automation
- seamless experience
- transformative solution
- cutting-edge innovation

### Fix
Replace generic wording with:
- category language
- contrast language
- workflow position
- buyer value
- control point language

Sharper beats louder.

---

## Red flag 5 — No painful problem

### What it looks like
The deck implies the product is helpful, but never clearly names a costly, emotionally resonant failure or bottleneck.

### Why it hurts
No pain means no urgency.
If the problem feels optional, the company feels optional.

### Signs in language
- “helps teams be more efficient”
- “improves productivity”
- “streamlines workflows”
- “makes it easier”

### Fix
Name the real failure mode vividly and concretely.
Good problems feel:
- expensive
- common
- easy to visualize
- strategically relevant

---

## Red flag 6 — No wedge

### What it looks like
The company appears to do too many things at once, or the initial entry point is unclear.

### Why it hurts
A deck without a wedge feels undisciplined.
Investors need to believe the company can win somewhere specific before it wins broadly.

### Signs in language
- too many workflows introduced at once
- the company sounds like an all-purpose platform on day one
- no sharp initial use case

### Fix
Define:
- the pain that is sharpest now
- the first adoption surface
- the expansion path from that narrow entry point

---

## Red flag 7 — Big market, weak mechanism

### What it looks like
The deck claims a huge opportunity but does not explain why this company captures meaningful spend.

### Why it hurts
Large numbers without mechanism feel lazy.
Investors care less about theoretical TAM than about credible capture logic.

### Signs in language
- giant TAM slide with no relation to product role
- “every developer is a customer”
- “trillion-dollar market” language
- market math replacing strategic argument

### Fix
Explain:
1. what market shift is happening
2. where spend already exists
3. why this company becomes a recurring budget line as the shift grows

---

## Red flag 8 — Competition denial

### What it looks like
The deck claims there are no competitors or dismisses adjacent players too casually.

### Why it hurts
It sounds naive.
Sophisticated investors expect a clear market map, not denial.

### Signs in language
- nobody does this
- no competition exists
- completely unique
- impossible to copy

### Fix
Acknowledge adjacent categories honestly.
Then explain why they are built for a different primary job.
The strongest competition slide defines the lane rather than pretending the race is empty.

---

## Red flag 9 — Weak moat language

### What it looks like
The deck claims defensibility but describes only features, effort, or speed of execution.

### Why it hurts
Features are not moats.
Work ethic is not a moat.
A moat must sound structural, compounding, or embedded.

### Signs in language
- best product
- more features than others
- moving faster
- strong team alone as primary moat

### Fix
Use moat language tied to:
- architecture
- workflow position
- data or artifact accumulation
- switching costs
- built-in-the-right-order advantages
- compounding memory or infrastructure

---

## Red flag 10 — No “why now”

### What it looks like
The deck explains the company but not why this is the right time for it.

### Why it hurts
Without timing, the company can feel either premature or late.

### Signs in language
- no macro shift slide
- no mention of why adoption is changing now
- no explanation of what recently became possible or necessary

### Fix
Answer at least one of:
- what changed in the market?
- what changed technologically?
- what changed in buyer behavior?
- why is this newly affordable, newly urgent, or newly open?

---

## Red flag 11 — The company sounds too small

### What it looks like
The deck communicates a narrow feature or utility but not a larger strategic destination.

### Why it hurts
Investors want to see a company that can grow into a large position, not remain a niche plugin forever.

### Signs in language
- no vision slide
- no platform expansion path
- no broader category claim
- overfocus on the first feature alone

### Fix
Show:
- the wedge
- the adjacent workflows
- the broader system role
- the larger category the company can own

Do not make it sound vague; make it sound like a logical expansion.

---

## Red flag 12 — The company sounds too broad

### What it looks like
The deck tries to claim a huge platform vision without showing a believable entry point.

### Why it hurts
This creates the opposite failure mode: grandiosity without grounding.

### Signs in language
- trying to do everything for everyone
- giant platform claims by slide 2
- broad vision with no clear beachhead

### Fix
Anchor the company in a sharp initial pain point first.
Then widen the story gradually.

---

## Red flag 13 — No buyer logic

### What it looks like
The deck talks about users but not buyers, budgets, or why money gets spent.

### Why it hurts
A useful product is not automatically a fundable company.
There must be a spending logic.

### Signs in language
- all benefits are personal convenience
- no mention of team, enterprise, platform, security, or governance implications
- no recurring spend framing

### Fix
Explain:
- who gets value
- who pays
- why they pay repeatedly
- what risk, cost, or leverage justifies spend

---

## Red flag 14 — Traction overstatement

### What it looks like
The deck exaggerates the strength of traction or product maturity.

### Why it hurts
Overclaiming destroys trust faster than modest numbers do.

### Signs in language
- implying product-market fit too early
- inflated wording around pilots or dogfooding
- vague adjectives replacing real evidence

### Fix
If traction is early, frame it as proof or de-risking:
- product exists
- workflows are live
- real bugs found
- users or teams are validating the need

Be specific and calm.

---

## Red flag 15 — Unsupported absolutes

### What it looks like
The deck uses brittle all-or-nothing claims that are easy to challenge.

### Why it hurts
One easy contradiction can make the entire deck feel unserious.

### Signs in language
- every developer uses AI
- no one else is doing this
- impossible to replicate
- enterprises must adopt this immediately

### Fix
Prefer:
- many / most / increasing / emerging / underserved / no clear leader

Calm precision sounds more credible than grand certainty.

---

## Red flag 16 — Forgettable headlines

### What it looks like
The slides contain technically correct text that nobody will remember after the meeting.

### Why it hurts
A deck competes for memory.
If no line sticks, no narrative sticks.

### Signs in language
- long explanatory headlines
- corporate wording
- headline and body saying the same thing weakly

### Fix
Use contrast, aphorism, or compression.
Aim for lines an investor could repeat later.
Examples:
- Software creation accelerated. Verification did not.
- The editor should not grade its own homework.
- AI writes the code. Prism decides when it is ready.

---

## Red flag 17 — Slide order breaks belief

### What it looks like
The deck contains decent content, but the sequence undermines persuasion.

### Why it hurts
Belief is cumulative. The order of information matters.

### Signs in structure
- TAM before painful problem
- competition before company role is clear
- ask before moat or proof
- roadmap before wedge
- product details before market shift or insight

### Fix
Rebuild the sequence so it moves through:
- category
- shift
- problem
- insight
- solution
- value
- wedge
- moat
- proof
- model
- vision
- ask

---

## Red flag 18 — The deck explains but does not incite

### What it looks like
A founder reviews the deck and says:
- this is accurate
- this is clear
- this explains the product

But not:
- this makes the company feel fundable
- this creates urgency
- this makes ownership attractive

### Why it hurts
Clarity is necessary but not sufficient.
The deck must convert understanding into desire.

### Fix
Ask directly:
- does this make owning the company feel strategically attractive?
- does this make the wedge, moat, and market pull obvious?
- does this sound like a company that grows stronger as the market grows?

If not, keep rewriting.

## Quick audit checklist

Use this fast review pass on any draft.

### Content red flags
- [ ] too many features, too little value
- [ ] no clear category line
- [ ] no painful problem
- [ ] no wedge
- [ ] no moat
- [ ] no why-now logic
- [ ] no buyer logic
- [ ] no expansion path
- [ ] no memorable lines

### Credibility red flags
- [ ] unsupported absolutes
- [ ] market claims with weak mechanism
- [ ] traction overstatement
- [ ] competition denial

### Structural red flags
- [ ] technical detail too early
- [ ] weak slide sequencing
- [ ] too much on individual slides
- [ ] product demo energy instead of venture energy

If multiple boxes are checked, the issue is usually not a sentence-level problem. It is a narrative problem.

Weak decks usually do not fail for lack of information.
They fail because they trigger the wrong investor reaction.

This skill exists to help catch that early.
