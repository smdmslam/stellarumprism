# Sequoia-Inspired Pitch Deck Template

Use this template when you want a deck to feel disciplined, founder-centric, timeless, credible, and structurally sharp in a way that reflects the public-facing style associated with Sequoia.

This template is not a copy of Sequoia’s proprietary materials.
It is a reusable interpretation of the recurring patterns visible in Sequoia’s founder guidance, public deck structure, and framework-oriented communication style.

---

## Core Brand Attributes

A Sequoia-inspired deck should feel:
- clear
- rigorous
- calm
- founder-focused
- enduring
- trustworthy
- high-standard
- strategically literate
- practical rather than theatrical
- persuasive through structure, not hype

It should not feel:
- flashy
- overdesigned
- trend-chasing
- slogan-heavy
- speculative without grounding
- cluttered
- theatrically visionary without business logic

---

## Core Design Principles

### 1. Clarity over ornament
Every slide should look like it was built to help a serious investor understand the business quickly.
Nothing should exist purely for decoration.

### 2. Structure creates confidence
The deck should feel organized, deliberate, and economically reasoned.
A clear sequence is part of the persuasion.

### 3. Calm authority beats performance
The deck should not beg for attention.
It should assume the content is strong enough to earn attention.

### 4. Founder judgment should be visible
The deck should imply that the team understands markets, tradeoffs, timing, and execution.

### 5. Precision is more persuasive than volume
Fewer, sharper claims are better than many loosely supported ones.

## Visual System

### Layout Rules
Use layouts that feel ordered, spacious, and easy to scan.

Preferred patterns:
- strong single-column slides
- clean two-column comparison slides
- simple section-divider slides
- matrix or framework slides when needed
- restrained chart slides
- sparse product slides with one clear takeaway

Avoid:
- mosaic-heavy layouts
- excessive cards everywhere
- ornamental sidebars
- busy background textures
- dramatic layered effects
- layouts with too many simultaneous focal points

### Spacing
- use generous margins
- keep consistent alignment
- allow slides to breathe
- reduce density before reducing font size
- maintain obvious hierarchy between headline, subpoint, and proof

### Typography Rules
Typography should feel sober, modern, and readable.

Use:
- clean sans-serif fonts
- strong but not aggressive headline weight
- medium-weight subheads
- highly readable body text
- limited type-scale complexity

Headline behavior:
- declarative
- businesslike
- direct
- often sentence-case
- occasionally short title-case if especially strong

Avoid:
- quirky display fonts
- oversized dramatic typography as the main style
- long multi-line headline paragraphs
- too many font weights or type treatments

### Color System
Keep the palette restrained.

Recommended behavior:
- white, off-white, charcoal, black, soft gray as the base
- one restrained accent color at most
- muted confidence rather than saturated branding theater
- charts and diagrams should use color functionally, not emotionally

Accent color usage should be sparse and deliberate:
- highlight one key number
- distinguish one category in a chart
- guide the eye in a framework

Avoid:
- rainbow systems
- hyper-saturated hero slides everywhere
- decorative gradients unless they are extremely subtle

### Imagery and Proof Elements
Images should support credibility, not decoration.

Good image types:
- product screenshots with clear purpose
- simple workflow diagrams
- customer or market proof visuals
- clean charts
- restrained iconography
- occasional founder/team photography if high quality and relevant

Avoid:
- stock-photo energy
- decorative abstract blobs
- image-heavy slides with weak business content
- screenshots dropped in without explanation

### Chart and Framework Style
This template supports simple business frameworks especially well.

Good patterns:
- 2x2 matrices
- process flows
- before/after comparisons
- wedge → expansion diagrams
- operating model diagrams
- market segmentation tables
- clean KPI snapshots

Frameworks should feel:
- legible
- simple
- analytical
- useful

## Messaging System

### Tone
The tone should feel:
- composed
- intelligent
- practical
- high-conviction without theatrics
- respectful of investor attention
- grounded in business reasoning

The deck should sound like:
- a strong founder who sees the market clearly
- a serious company being built with discipline
- a team that understands how category value is created

It should not sound like:
- ad copy
- hype-heavy startup theater
- vague mission language without operating substance
- product marketing pretending to be company strategy

### Headline Rules
Each slide headline should make a real claim.

Good Sequoia-style headline traits:
- concise
- credible
- strategic
- often tied to market truth, user pain, or company leverage

Examples:
- Security review is becoming a prerequisite to software adoption
- Existing tools detect issues, but do not close the verification loop
- Our wedge is the highest-friction point in the workflow
- Timing matters because AI adoption has outpaced trust infrastructure
- We begin with one bottleneck and expand into the system of record

Avoid headlines like:
- Reimagining the Future of Intelligent Collaboration
- The Next Generation Platform for Modern Teams Everywhere
- Revolutionizing an Entire Industry with AI-Native Excellence

### Copy Rules
Body copy should be:
- short
- precise
- concrete
- low-fluff
- supportive of the headline rather than redundant

Use:
- short bullets
- simple business language
- evidence-oriented phrases
- careful distinctions

Avoid:
- paragraph blocks unless absolutely necessary
- grandiose wording
- repeated synonyms for emphasis
- startup clichés

## Narrative Arc

A Sequoia-inspired narrative should feel logically earned.
The investor should feel that each slide answers the next obvious question.

### Recommended story flow
1. What is the company and why does it matter?
2. What painful problem exists?
3. Why is this problem increasing now?
4. Why are existing approaches insufficient?
5. What is the product or system?
6. Why is this wedge strategically strong?
7. How does the company expand over time?
8. Why is the business credible and defensible?
9. Why is the market large enough?
10. Why is this team suited to win?
11. What happens financially if the company executes?
12. What is the long-term company outcome?

### Narrative quality bar
The deck should create belief through:
- order
- judgment
- evidence
- coherence

Not through:
- intensity alone
- too many features
- raw ambition without business framing

## Slide-by-Slide Template Structure

Below is the recommended Sequoia-inspired structure.
You can compress or expand it, but preserve the logic.

---

### Slide 1 — Company Purpose
**Goal:** State what the company is in one sentence.

**Headline style:** Direct and simple.

**Template:**
- `[Company] helps [customer] achieve [important outcome] by solving [specific painful problem].`

**Include:**
- one-line company definition
- optional sub-line with category or wedge
- very light supporting proof if needed

**Do:**
- make it clear quickly
- sound inevitable, not ornamental

**Don’t:**
- open with mission poetry
- make the investor decode the business

---

### Slide 2 — Problem
**Goal:** Show the painful, costly, or strategically important problem.

**Include:**
- the workflow failure
- who experiences it
- why it matters economically or operationally
- what breaks under current conditions

**Strong patterns:**
- 3-point pain summary
- before/current state comparison
- workflow friction map

**Headline examples:**
- Teams ship faster than they can verify safely
- Critical decisions still rely on fragmented manual workflows
- The current workflow breaks at the point of scale

---

### Slide 3 — Why Now
**Goal:** Explain why timing has changed.

**Include:**
- macro shift
- enabling technology shift
- behavioral adoption shift
- regulatory or operational pressure if relevant

**Strong patterns:**
- 3 stacked timing drivers
- timeline with inflection point
- market change + infrastructure gap

**Headline examples:**
- Adoption has accelerated faster than trust infrastructure
- The market changed; the workflow did not
- A new behavior is now mainstream, but the system around it is missing

---

### Slide 4 — Solution
**Goal:** Show the core product or system clearly.

**Include:**
- what the product does
- how it fits into the workflow
- why it is meaningfully different
- the main user or buyer value

**Preferred style:**
- simple diagram
- annotated screenshot
- 3-step process

**Headline examples:**
- We turn a fragmented workflow into one verifiable system
- The product sits at the decision point where trust is required
- One platform replaces a chain of manual review steps

---

### Slide 5 — Why Existing Alternatives Fall Short
**Goal:** Show structural insufficiency in current solutions.

**Include:**
- categories of alternatives
- what each does well
- where each fails structurally
- why this gap persists

**Preferred style:**
- comparison table
- solution-category framework
- “good for X, weak for Y” matrix

**Rule:**
Be fair and credible.
Do not pretend there is no competition.

**Headline examples:**
- Existing tools solve fragments, not the full decision loop
- The market has point solutions, not system ownership
- Alternatives optimize for visibility, not resolution

---

### Slide 6 — Wedge
**Goal:** Show the sharp initial entry point.

**Include:**
- the first urgent use case
- why buyers adopt here first
- why this wedge naturally expands

**Preferred style:**
- one wedge box + adjacent expansion paths
- economic pain / urgency / buyer ownership framing

**Headline examples:**
- We start where the pain is highest and the buyer is clear
- Our wedge is the bottleneck every team already feels
- The first use case is narrow enough to win and broad enough to expand

---

### Slide 7 — Expansion Path
**Goal:** Explain how the company grows beyond the initial use case.

**Include:**
- adjacent workflows
- product surface expansion
- data / system-of-record advantage
- account expansion logic

**Preferred style:**
- staged roadmap
- concentric expansion map
- workflow ownership diagram

**Headline examples:**
- The wedge expands into the operating layer around it
- We begin with one decision point and grow into the system of record
- Adoption starts narrow but compounds across the workflow

---

### Slide 8 — Product / Proof
**Goal:** Show evidence that the product is real and useful.

**Include any combination of:**
- product screenshots
- user workflow
- early traction
- pilot usage
- retained usage behavior
- deployment proof
- customer examples

**Rule:**
Choose 1-3 proof points that reduce risk.
Do not overcrowd this slide.

**Headline examples:**
- Early usage shows the workflow is real and repeatable
- Teams already use the platform where trust matters most
- Product proof reduces execution risk

---

### Slide 9 — Business Model
**Goal:** Show how the company makes money and why the model can scale.

**Include:**
- pricing basis
- buyer
- sales motion
- expansion logic
- retention or recurring revenue logic

**Preferred style:**
- simple revenue model graphic
- pricing + expansion table
- recurring spend explanation

**Headline examples:**
- Revenue scales with workflow ownership
- The business model aligns to recurring operational value
- Land with one team, expand with the system

---

### Slide 10 — Market Size
**Goal:** Show that the opportunity is large enough and logically connected to the wedge.

**Include:**
- who pays
- how many potential buyers exist
- why the wedge leads into broader spend
- how the category expands over time

**Preferred style:**
- bottom-up logic if possible
- segmented market view
- wedge-to-platform market framing

**Headline examples:**
- The wedge is narrow; the market it opens is not
- A single pain point leads into a broad recurring spend category
- The category is larger than the first product surface

---

### Slide 11 — Defensibility / Why We Win
**Goal:** Explain why the company can become enduring.

**Include:**
- workflow control point
- data advantage
- integration depth
- switching costs
- product complexity
- trust layer position
- brand or distribution flywheel if applicable

**Preferred style:**
- defensibility stack
- moat pillars
- workflow control diagram

**Headline examples:**
- The moat comes from workflow position, not feature count
- We become harder to replace as the system becomes trusted
- Defensibility increases as verification history accumulates

---

### Slide 12 — Team
**Goal:** Show that the team is credible for this problem.

**Include:**
- relevant founder insight
- domain experience
- technical capability
- prior execution signals
- why this team saw the problem early

**Rule:**
Keep it factual.
Avoid inflated self-description.

**Headline examples:**
- This team understands the problem from the inside
- Founder-market fit is visible in both product and execution
- The company is built by operators who know where the workflow breaks

---

### Slide 13 — Financials / Plan
**Goal:** Show disciplined thinking about the business.

**Include:**
- growth assumptions
- revenue trajectory
- key operating drivers
- major spending logic
- milestones tied to capital

**Preferred style:**
- clean table
- simple chart
- assumption notes

**Rule:**
Be sober.
Precision and logic matter more than maximal optimism.

**Headline examples:**
- The plan is ambitious, but the operating logic is clear
- Capital accelerates a repeatable go-to-market motion
- Growth is driven by workflow expansion and recurring spend

---

### Slide 14 — Vision / End State
**Goal:** End with the larger company outcome.

**Include:**
- what the company becomes if it wins
- why that future matters structurally
- how the wedge leads there

**Tone:**
Aspirational but earned.

**Headline examples:**
- We are building the trust layer for AI-native software adoption
- What begins as a workflow tool becomes core operating infrastructure
- The long-term company owns the system around this decision point

---

### Slide 15 — Ask
**Goal:** State the raise and intended use clearly.

**Include:**
- amount raising
- what it funds
- major milestones enabled
- approximate timeline or runway if useful

**Headline examples:**
- This round funds product expansion, go-to-market, and key proof milestones
- We are raising to scale the wedge into the category

---

## Reusable Slide Rules

Across the whole deck:
- one key claim per slide
- one dominant visual idea per slide
- make the next question feel naturally answered by the next slide
- keep copy lower than your instinct says
- avoid decorative complexity
- avoid anything that feels like startup theater
- use proof carefully and early enough to sustain belief
- prefer strategic calm over intensity

---

## Do / Don’t Guide

### Do
- sound thoughtful
- make business logic explicit
- show timing clearly
- define the wedge precisely
- connect wedge to expansion
- show credible competition framing
- make financial and market claims legible
- use diagrams and matrices when they clarify thinking

### Don’t
- oversaturate slides with design effects
- rely on grandiose branding language
- exaggerate uniqueness carelessly
- make every slide a manifesto
- confuse product detail with company value
- hide weak logic behind beautiful design

## Usage Notes

This template works especially well for companies that want to signal:
- founder quality
- strategic rigor
- category clarity
- durable business thinking
- credible ambition

It is a strong fit for:
- infrastructure
- enterprise software
- developer tools
- workflow systems
- trust / compliance / verification products
- operational platforms

It is a weaker fit for decks that depend on:
- playful consumer branding
- fashion-forward visual identity
- highly emotional storytelling
- maximalist creative expression

---

## Optional Variant Modes

### 1. Sequoia-Minimal
Use even fewer visual elements.
Very sparse, text-light, highly disciplined.
Best for elite founder / enterprise / infrastructure narratives.

### 2. Sequoia-Framework
Lean harder into diagrams, matrices, operating models, and market logic.
Best for technically complex or operationally nuanced companies.

### 3. Sequoia-Product-Proof
Use when product screenshots and workflow proof are unusually important.
Still restrained, but with slightly more visible product evidence.

---

## Final Principle

A Sequoia-inspired pitch deck should not try to impress by looking expensive.
It should impress by making the company appear:
- inevitable in logic
- disciplined in execution
- credible in ambition
- clear in market position

That is the template system.
