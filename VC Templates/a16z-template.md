# a16z-Inspired Pitch Deck Template

A structured reference template for creating pitch decks inspired by a16z's current editorial, tech-forward, thesis-driven brand style.

## 1. Brand Attributes

Use these attributes as the style filter for every deck:

- Bold
- Analytical
- Future-facing
- Contrarian
- Premium
- Clear
- Technical but readable
- Editorial, not corporate

## 2. Core Design Principles

- Every slide should make a claim.
- Prioritize argument over decoration.
- Use clean layouts with strong hierarchy.
- Make the deck feel like a sharp market thesis, not a generic sales presentation.
- Prefer proof elements over filler visuals.
- Keep the tone confident, informed, and strategic.

## 3. Visual System

### A. Overall Aesthetic

The deck should feel like:

- a premium tech editorial
- an investor thesis memo
- a future-focused market briefing

Avoid making it feel like:

- a playful startup explainer
- a consulting slide dump
- an overly flashy marketing deck

### B. Layout Rules

- Use one dominant idea per slide.
- Keep strong margins and generous whitespace.
- Prefer left-aligned text.
- Use asymmetry carefully, but keep slides disciplined.
- Limit each slide to one hero visual or proof block.
- Avoid dense multi-column clutter unless absolutely necessary.

### C. Typography Rules

- Use modern sans-serif typography.
- Headlines should be large, bold, and assertive.
- Body copy should be concise and easy to scan.
- Use weight and spacing for emphasis rather than decorative effects.
- Avoid long paragraph blocks.

### D. Color System

#### Body Slides
- White or very light neutral background
- Black / dark gray text
- One accent color only

#### Divider / Hero Slides
- Dark graphite / black background
- High-contrast white text
- Optional subtle gradient or abstract tech texture
- Accent color used sparingly for emphasis

### E. Imagery and Proof Elements

Prefer:

- product screenshots
- charts
- market maps
- architecture diagrams
- workflow visuals
- abstract high-tech textures for divider slides

Avoid:

- generic stock photos
- cartoon-style icon sets
- decorative filler images
- cluttered collage visuals

## 4. Messaging System

### A. Tone

The writing should feel:

- confident
- intelligent
- concise
- market-aware
- strategic
- forward-looking

### B. Headline Rules

Every slide headline should make a claim, not just name a section.

Examples:

- AI is turning software from tools into operators
- A services-heavy market is becoming a software market
- Teams still manage this workflow in fragmented systems
- The wedge is narrow, but the platform opportunity is massive

### C. Narrative Arc

The ideal deck story should follow this flow:

1. A major shift is happening
2. Existing systems are broken
3. This product is built for the new environment
4. The market is large because the shift is structural
5. The company has a strong wedge and durable advantage
6. Traction proves the thesis
7. The long-term vision is category-defining

## 5. Slide-by-Slide Template Structure

## Slide 1: Cover

### Goal
Introduce the company with a sharp thesis.

### Include
- company name
- one-line description or thesis
- optional logo
- subtle product image or abstract visual

### Style Notes
- minimal text
- strong spacing
- premium first impression

### Example Headline
- The infrastructure layer for autonomous finance

---

## Slide 2: Why Now

### Goal
Explain the market or technology shift creating urgency.

### Include
- 2-4 macro shifts
- one chart, stat, or supporting visual
- concise explanation of why timing matters now

### Example Headlines
- AI is turning software from tools into operators
- Regulatory change is opening a category that could not exist five years ago

---

## Slide 3: Problem

### Goal
Define the pain point clearly and concretely.

### Include
- who experiences the problem
- current workflow pain
- why existing tools fail

### Example Headlines
- Mission-critical decisions are still made in fragmented systems
- Teams rely on manual coordination for a workflow that should be automated

---

## Slide 4: Insight

### Goal
Show the non-obvious truth behind the opportunity.

### Include
- contrarian insight
- what incumbents miss
- why the timing and wedge now make sense

### Example Headlines
- The opportunity is not better software — it is workflow replacement
- The real unlock is not access to data, but action on top of it

---

## Slide 5: Solution

### Goal
Present the company clearly in one sentence and one system view.

### Include
- one-sentence company description
- product architecture or workflow diagram
- key differentiator

### Example Headline
- A workflow-native system built for real-time financial operations

---

## Slide 6: Product

### Goal
Show what the product actually does.

### Include
- screenshots
- 3-step workflow
- key capabilities
- optional annotations

### Style Notes
- keep explanations tight
- let the product visual do the work

### Example Headline
- The product automates decisions, execution, and reconciliation in one loop

---

## Slide 7: Market

### Goal
Frame the market as large and structurally important.

### Include
- category framing
- TAM/SAM/SOM if useful
- market shift argument
- reasons the market expands over time

### Example Headlines
- A $20B services market is becoming a software market
- This category expands as workflows become machine-executable

---

## Slide 8: Competition / Landscape

### Goal
Explain why the current market structure is vulnerable and why this company wins.

### Include
- competitive landscape map or category comparison
- why incumbents fail
- why alternatives are incomplete
- why your wedge matters

### Example Headlines
- Incumbents optimize tasks. We automate the system of work.
- The market is crowded with tools, but empty at the workflow layer

---

## Slide 9: Go-to-Market

### Goal
Show how the company acquires customers and expands.

### Include
- ICP
- entry wedge
- acquisition channel
- expansion path
- sales motion or product-led motion

### Example Headlines
- We enter through one painful workflow and expand into the full operating stack
- Distribution starts with compliance teams and expands across finance operations

---

## Slide 10: Traction

### Goal
Provide proof that the thesis is already working.

### Include
- growth metrics
- retention / usage / revenue quality
- key logos if appropriate
- one primary chart

### Style Notes
- use no more than 3-4 metrics
- emphasize signal, not volume

### Example Headlines
- Usage is compounding faster than headcount
- Early customers are expanding from point use cases to system adoption

---

## Slide 11: Defensibility

### Goal
Explain why this company becomes stronger over time.

### Include
- data advantage
- workflow lock-in
- network effects
- proprietary systems
- brand or distribution advantages

### Example Headlines
- The moat comes from embedded workflow, not feature depth
- Every deployment increases the intelligence of the system

---

## Slide 12: Team

### Goal
Show why this team is uniquely positioned to win.

### Include
- founder-market fit
- domain expertise
- execution credibility
- selective prior logos / wins

### Example Headlines
- Built by operators who lived the workflow firsthand
- The team combines domain depth, technical execution, and distribution insight

---

## Slide 13: Vision

### Goal
Show the long-term category outcome.

### Include
- what the company becomes in 5-10 years
- category-defining ambition
- strategic end-state

### Example Headlines
- Today: workflow software. Tomorrow: the operating layer for autonomous enterprises.
- We start with one workflow and become the control system for the category.

---

## Slide 14: Raise

### Goal
Close with a clear ask and what it unlocks.

### Include
- raise amount
- use of funds
- milestones
- why this is the right moment

### Example Headlines
- We are raising to turn early pull into category leadership
- This round funds product depth, distribution scale, and market capture

## 6. Reusable Slide Rules

Use these rules across every deck:

- One core idea per slide
- Maximum 3-5 bullets per slide
- Keep subtext short and scannable
- Every slide should have a clear takeaway
- Charts must support a strategic claim
- Prefer screenshots and diagrams over decorative visuals
- Remove anything that does not strengthen the thesis

## 7. Do / Don't Guide

### Do
- Write headlines as arguments
- Use real proof points
- Make the market feel inevitable
- Balance technical depth with readability
- Use whitespace to create authority
- Let visuals clarify the message

### Don't
- Use generic section titles as headlines
- Add unnecessary icons or filler graphics
- Overload slides with text
- Use multiple accent colors
- Make every slide visually loud
- Confuse jargon with insight

## 8. Sample Headline Bank

Use or adapt these when creating new decks:

- Software is moving from interface to execution
- The category is opening because the workflow is now machine-readable
- Legacy systems were built for recordkeeping, not real-time action
- The wedge is operational pain, but the outcome is platform control
- Early adoption proves this is not a feature — it is a system transition
- What starts as automation becomes infrastructure
- The real moat is participation in the workflow itself
