Slide intent

Here is the practical meaning behind each title so you can rename them intelligently for your story.

Slide purpose

What it needs to do

Cover / Company Snapshot

Say what the company is in one line and anchor the category fast.

Problem

Show the pain clearly and make it feel expensive, frequent, and urgent.

Why Now

Explain why this market moment creates an opening now, not later.

Solution

State your answer in a simple, memorable sentence.

Product / Demo

Prove the product is real and understandable, not conceptual.

Competition

Show why your approach wins against status quo and direct alternatives.

Business Model

Explain how money comes in and how this scales.

Traction

Provide proof that users, usage, or revenue validate the story.

Team

Convince investors this team is uniquely suited to win.

Ask

Show how much capital you want and what milestone it unlocks.

Suggested renaming examples

For example, instead of a generic “Problem” slide, you could retitle it “AI Coding Tools Confidently Guess Wrong.” Instead of “Solution,” you could use “A Terminal IDE That Investigates Before It Concludes,” which preserves the classic slide purpose while making your product thesis unmistakable.

A strong investor-grade naming set for your deck might be:

- The Cost of Guessing in AI-Assisted Coding.

- Verification Before Generation.

- The Diagnostic Truth Layer.

- From Root-Cause Detection to Code Execution.

- A New Interface for Serious Builders.

The strongest decks stay short, with each slide answering one investor question and building a clean narrative rather than trying to explain everything at once.

The outline above is solid. These are notes I keep coming back to when actually building decks for an investor read, not a stage talk.

1) ONE INVESTOR QUESTION PER SLIDE. Every slide must answer exactly one question the reader is already asking. If you can't write that question on the page in five words, the slide is doing too much. The mapping for an infra/dev-tools deck:

   - Cover                  -> What is this?
   - Problem                -> Why does this matter?
   - Why now                -> Why is the moment now?
   - Principle / Insight    -> What did the founders see that others missed?
   - Solution               -> What did you build?
   - Substrate / Architecture -> Why is this hard to copy?
   - Consumers / Surfaces   -> What can users actually do today?
   - Demo                   -> Is it real?
   - ICP                    -> Who pays?
   - Market                 -> How big does it get?
   - Competition            -> Why don't the giants just do this?
   - Durability / Moat      -> Why does the value compound?
   - Business model         -> How does money come in?
   - Traction               -> Does anyone want it?
   - Roadmap                -> What's the next 12 months?
   - Team                   -> Why you?
   - Ask                    -> What unlocks the next milestone?

2) HERO LINE PER SLIDE. Every slide gets one sentence in the largest type that, alone, carries the slide's message. Investors skim. The hero line is what they remember. Three to nine words is the sweet spot. Examples that work:

   - "AI editors confidently guess wrong."
   - "Verification before generation."
   - "The compiler is the source of truth."
   - "Switching costs come free with usage."

   The supporting bullets exist to back the hero line up, not to compete with it.

3) DENSITY BUDGET. A working slide has at most: 1 hero line, 3-5 supporting bullets of <= 12 words each, optional one diagram or screenshot. If you're past that, split the slide. The deck overall should be readable in ~6 minutes silent and ~12 minutes presented; that gates total slide count to about 14-20.

4) THE STORY SPINE. The deck is one argument, not seventeen. A clean spine for a verification-substrate dev-tool company:

   Pain -> Insight -> Architecture -> Surfaces -> Defensibility -> Trajectory -> Ask

   Each section should hand off to the next: the pain motivates the insight, the insight forces the architecture, the architecture enables the surfaces, the surfaces compound into defensibility, defensibility justifies the trajectory, the trajectory sets up the ask. If a slide doesn't sit on this spine, cut it.

5) INFRASTRUCTURE-PRODUCT TRAPS. Specific to technical / dev-tools decks; investor friction comes from:

   - JARGON CASCADES. Substrate, tool loop, evidence grading, deterministic checks - all useful internally, all opaque on a slide. Lead with the user-visible outcome ("every finding is backed by a real compiler error") and let the term-of-art appear once, defined inline.
   - DEMO RISK. A live demo that depends on a network call or a model provider WILL fail in front of an investor. Use a recorded GIF or a static screenshot of a real run. Save the live demo for second meetings.
   - FEATURE LISTING WITHOUT THESIS. Listing 7 substrate cells looks impressive to engineers and reads as 'unclear product' to investors. Group features under the principle they enforce; never let a slide be a literal product changelog.
   - OVER-CLAIMING DEFENSIBILITY. "Network effects" and "data moat" are red flags from a 30-day-old product. Be specific about the moat type and what concretely accrues over time (audit history, project skills, accumulated wiring-gap dataset, etc.).
   - UNDER-CLAIMING TRACTION. Self-dogfooding on real production codebases IS traction, especially for dev tools where the founder is the ICP. Quantify it (lines of code under audit, tests passing, bugs surfaced, real users) instead of leaving it implicit.

6) WHAT TO SHOW VS WHAT TO TELL. Show: a real findings list with severity + confidence + source labels; a one-line CLI invocation; a screenshot of the file tree + Problems panel; the markdown report's frontmatter. Tell: the architecture, the moat, the math. Do not show architecture diagrams to investors who don't already believe in your category - they read as 'engineering-led'.

7) THE 30-SECOND TEST. After the deck is done, hand it to someone who hasn't heard the pitch and time how long it takes them to articulate, in their words, (a) what the company sells, (b) who buys it, (c) why now, (d) why this team. If any of those takes more than 30 seconds of skimming, you have a slide problem, not a typography problem.

These additions don't replace the outline above; they're the rules of the road when actually populating it for a specific company.
